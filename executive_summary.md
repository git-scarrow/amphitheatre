# Executive Summary — Petoskey Pit Amphitheater & Civic Event Garden

**Bayfront Park, Petoskey, Emmet County, Michigan** — a partial-fan outdoor amphitheater and
civic event garden set into the "Petoskey Pit," a closed-surface depression beside Little
Traverse Bay and the Bear River mouth.

> **PLANNING-GRADE — NOT STAMPED ENGINEERING.** Every figure below is a planning estimate
> from public LiDAR plus labelled assumptions. No soil, groundwater, contamination, or
> storm-sewer field data has been collected. **The hydrology, hydraulics, geotechnical, and
> structural design must be PE-stamped before construction.** See `gating_dossier.md`.

---

## The concept in one paragraph

The bowl is turned into a venue from the **bottom up**: a stormwater treatment cell at the
floor, a raised event floor above the design flood pool, terraced seating climbing the steep
south/southeast wall, and a garden ring on the gentle east and open bayward flanks. The
audience sits on the **S/SE arc and faces northwest (azimuth 330°) toward the bay and the
evening light**, looking out over the stage and a reflecting treatment pool to Little Traverse
Bay. The event floor elevation is set by **stormwater and groundwater logic, not by the
existing low point**, and the bowl bottom becomes a water-quality feature rather than occupied
space — exactly the design intent.

## What the analysis established (planning-grade)

- **The venue floor is set at 613.0 ft NAVD88** (recommended band 612.0–613.0). This sits ~1.7
  ft above the 100-yr design pool (611.31 ft, worst-soil case) and clears the 500-yr check
  (611.78 ft). The bowl bottom is **left at ~609.1 ft as a bioretention/treatment cell** — not
  excavated.
- **The bowl is hugely oversized as detention.** It holds **5.69 ac-ft** to its natural spill
  at 618.04 ft; even the 500-yr storm in the worst soil uses only ~17% of capacity. The design
  is **inflow-limited, not capacity-limited.**
- **Seating: 30 terraced rows, a ±30° fan, ~2,190 compact (18 in) or ~1,790 generous (22 in)
  seats — a geometric planning estimate, _not_ a code occupant load or operational event cap**
  (those require an egress/code analysis and a per-event permit; see `package/05_seating/seat_count.md`).
  Sightlines meet a 90 mm C-value target in all rows on the proposed grade; the bowl's ~16% rim
  means **engineered ADA ramps are required** (no natural accessible route exists).
- **Earthwork balances on site.** Cut 3,149 / fill 2,670 / **net −479 CY** (a mild surplus) at
  the chosen floor — **cut-dominant across the whole floor band, never needing imported fill**.
  A separate ~1,700 CY emergency-spillway rim cut brings the combined total to ~2,200 CY of
  net export, all retainable/usable on site.
- **A controlled outlet + armored emergency spillway** convert the closed bowl into a safe,
  metered-release basin: forebay → bioretention WQ cell (WQv 0.094 ac-ft) → control structure
  (orifice + weir) → outfall, plus a **612.0-ft emergency spillway** that engages below the
  occupied floor and clears the 500-yr surface storm with 0.80 ft of freeboard.
- **View and glare are separable.** The brief assumed the bay view and the low-evening-sun
  glare share the NW axis. The sun geometry shows otherwise: the **sun never sets on the 330°
  view axis** (the most northerly sunset is az 305° at the summer solstice). Glare lives in a
  **W–WNW sector (≤308°), offset ~25–40° to the audience's left.** Recommendation: keep the
  **315°–345° bay-view corridor open** and put an **18–26 ft glare grove + an asymmetric WNW
  stage-backdrop wing in the 280°–308° sector** — which also shades the treatment cell.

## The receiving water drives the stormwater strategy

The bowl overflows toward the **Bear River mouth / Little Traverse Bay — a restored coldwater
fishery.** The treatment train is built for **infiltrate-first, shade, sediment-trap, and
energy-dissipate**, and assumes EGLE stormwater, Part 91 erosion/sediment, Part 303 wetland,
and NPDES/CGP review will all apply.

## The honest uncertainties (what could move the design)

Four field unknowns dominate, in priority order:

1. **The City storm-sewer network** — the only input that could change the answer. LiDAR
   cannot see piped flow; if the network routes a large area into the pit, the flood pool,
   floor, and spillway must be re-derived.
2. **Seasonal high groundwater** near the bowl floor — likely the elevation-setting input; a
   local perched table would force a permanent pool.
3. **Infiltration rate + SE-wall slope stability** — set the treatment regime (underdrain?)
   and whether the steep wall is a routine retaining wall or a major geotechnical cost.
4. **Boundary/shoreline extent + wetland delineation** — set what is buildable and where it
   can legally discharge.

All field, permitting, and approval prerequisites — each with an owner and purpose — are in
**`gating_dossier.md`**. The full georeferenced layout, grading plan, sections, tables,
seat count, and sun/view study are assembled and indexed in **`package/INDEX.md`**.

## Datum note

All elevations are **NAVD88 (Geoid12A), international feet** (the brief's "US survey feet" is
corrected — Michigan uses international feet). Bay level (~581 ft IGLD85) was reconciled to
**~581.4 ft NAVD88** using an assumed **+0.40 ft** offset that is **unconfirmed** and must be
verified via NOAA VDatum/NGS before any flood-elevation decision.
